> How would you introduce yourself at a party, to somebody that didn’t know you or your work?

It’s difficult to specify, I guess, the style of photography that I do – because you can just say “photography", but I think it’s like a blend of documentary and fine art; and I think that it is, it lands somewhere in between the two, kind of blending the two a bit.

There is like a mystery to it, an air of un-awareness between truth and fiction; and I think that leaving that up to interpretation strengthens the work.

it feels like archeology to me, in a way.

I guess that’s the best way to put it. At a party, I don’t know if that would be the best way to introduce myself, like, a casual thing to say (laughs), “I’m at the intersection of…”, you know (laughs). But that’s the best description I could find. It feels like archaeology to me, in a way.

✹

what I do, what I’m interested in, in terms of like visual stuff, is like, isolated communities, the concept of decaying Americana, and kind of, the breakdown of society in some way.

I'm trying to capture places that are just outside of the view of most people – stories that nobody really considers, that have been overlooked for many years, of which there are only fragments left over.

✹

> Nowadays, the social media space seems to be mostly fixated on video content. Has this ever made you question your choice for still photography?

Aa lot of people have like tried to coerce me into video work, and I think there could have been a lot of potential work if I had pivoted to video – which is like, such a buzzword to say now, “pivoting to video”. It leaves a bad taste in my mouth, just saying it.

But yeah, I held my ground on photography and stills for two reasons: one of them being that I’m not very good at video. I’m too picky, and I think that there are too many variables with video that I think won’t capture. I think video may be too truthful, in a lot of ways, and I think that’s not what I want my work to be.

I want my work to be open to interpretation, and I want to keep it a little bit removed from reality. If you give your audience everything, it kind of takes the enjoyment out of it, in a way.

It’s like, I am doing documentary work in a way, but unless I’m shooting for an actual documentary publication, I don’t see the point in being hyper-truthful, I don’t think that’s a requirement. I think that what makes it art in the first place is that the interpretation is varied by everyone.

And the other reason why I haven’t done video is that, I think that the stories that I’m trying to tell, they're just better suited for stills.

I mean, I love cinema, I love film, I love all mediums of visual art, and there’s nothing wrong with video work; and I think there are fantastic people doing fantastic work – but I just think that the subject matter that I’m focused on, and the kind of stories that I’m trying to tell, I feel like I have a lot more minute control, by working with pictures.

And I think I can kind of structure it to be “everything in one”. I want everything to be seen as a singular thing, and also – it’s more flexible, I guess.

Most of your photography focuses, as you said, on left-over rural areas or communities. Why does this attract you as a theme and a location?

I think rural places are very important, because that’s the forefront of like, a lot of what’s happening in our American society. That’s where things kind of like bubble under the surface, that’s where people are a lot angrier, and people are more frustrated with like, the position that they find themselves in.

As we’ve grown more polarized rapidly, within the past decade or so, as everyone moved to the cities and people continued to like, kind of congregate in singular places, everything’s become more like a bubble.

I grew up in a very rural area, so I think I'm really familiar with the attitude and the energy there, and I think that is really important to kind of document what’s really happening.

✹

the people that are affected most drastically or most immediately, kind of like the canary in the coal mine, are people that live in disenfranchised areas, isolated communities, that you know, live without government assistance in a lot of ways.

I think it’s really important to me, not only because I have a personal affection to them because of my past, but also I think they’re kind of the harbinger of what our society is turning into, in one way or another.

✹

People that live in rural communities are vaguely marginalized, in a certain way; there are a number of assumptions about how they act, and how they behave, and they’re kind of seen as ignorant, and gullible – at least that’s the case for the United States, and most of Western society even.

I know this is something that happens in Europe as well, and I think that it’s really important to kind of shed light on that negative stereotype.

i have a deep fascination for how varied and diverse our country is.

I think the last thing I want is for my work to be this kind of “gawking”, or this tourism, because it’s not that – I never intend for it to be like “look at how weird this is, look at how weird these rural people are”, because these communities exist, these places are real, and people live within them.

By shedding light on them, from an artistic perspective, and kind of like digging under the surface a little bit, like just scratching the surface, you can find such a rich, like, unique culture, that has developed by being isolated. It’s kind of like a petri dish.

I just have a deep fascination for how varied and diverse our country is.

How much do you alter what you see, in order to create your images?

I do sometimes leave touches and traces of myself in the work, in the way that I do alter things occasionally, for the art's sake; but I try my best to tell the story that I see there as I find it.

When I go into, like, an abandoned house, maybe, as the perfect example, to where it’s a completely sealed ecosystem, I want to document it as archeologically as I can; so altering it would be kind of, removing that natural touch to it.

I never destroy or worsen the environments that I go through in any way, but I will composite my stories occasionally, maybe from various photographs into a singular one.

Because I think that’s my pickiness, that’s the big thing where I have a disagreement with a lot of journalistic photography, or at least purely documentary work –I think there are places where there is a need for that.

I feel like I’m like collecting everything and then like, by putting it together in a singular image, that’s a better documentation for the whole space, the feeling, the experience.

I try to shoot in a way where – I like to shoot with telephoto lenses, in order to compress everything, and to bring everything into this kind of like, storybook-look to it.

With like the advent of AI, everyone started cataloging my work like that, and questioning whether I was using AI software to achieve some of my photographs.

First of all, that’s deeply insulting (laughs), but I think that what that shows is that I’m doing my job, in a way. I’m capturing something that seems unbelievable, the fact that this place exists, in a way where it feels like there’s no way it could exist in real life.

In reality, however, the amount of editing that I do to my images is minimal, it is very light.

> Considering the importance of location in your projects, and how large the USA is as a country, how do you find places to go?

After all this time shooting this kind of projects, and the amount of research that I’ve been putting into it, I know now almost instinctively what to look for, in order to dig out more, and speed up the process – I know that there are regions of the United States that I’ve always been drawn to. It’s like magnetic, almost.

It’s always been about this kind of dense wilderness with just kind of pockets of human civilization; I am drawn to places that were once prosperous, but no longer are.

I grew up in a logging town in south Oregon, that’s a good example of that – where a town that was once prosperous, after the lumber market crashed, all the people left, all the money left, and now it’s just decaying there, existing there, and that’s kind of what I’ve been looking for, elsewhere.

When people used to think of America, for decades, it was all about the prosperous wheatfields, and the farms, and the countryside, and things like that. But that’s no longer the reality.

I can drive by a town and can just kind of like scope out, like a certain amount of abandoned houses, or I can like see a level of like kind of emptyness to it – it’s like a learned vision for it, I’m obsessed with it (laughs), and that’s why I’m searching for these things.

For example, this past February I went to the deep south, I went to the Mississippi delta – which is historically ripe with a lot of turmoil; there is something about the deep south in general, that I’m drawn to as well. It has a level of “haunted” feeling to it.

I think that was a good example of the whole process: I was going to a place that I had never been to before, but I knew that I had to go there because the potential for what I could find was high.

I went to Saskatchewan and Manitoba, because I know that that areas may be interesting, and North Dakota is probably one of my most favourite states to visit, because it’s the least visited state, basically (laughs) by the standards of American travel.

> Do you think all of this may have something to do with the nature of the American people itself?

I shot a series called American Poetry over the course of the entire Trump presidency, from like the year before he got elected, to the end of its presidency; and you could just, in documenting just signs, just roadsigns that people would make, you could see all of this.

Americans are obviously outspoken, we can never seem to shut up about America (laughs) but it’s definitely – you could see people getting angrier, you could see people getting more and more divisive.

And what he kind of inspired in our society, that it was always there, we can no longer pretend that it wasn’t, is that America is kind of a violent place.

He fostered that feeling, and kind of dug it out a lot, and you can really really get to see it unfold into like a certain level of extremism, and people’s beliefs reached this point of delusion where it’s weird talking to people nowadays.

I can go to rural areas and talk to people, and they can be the sweetest people, the nicest people you could ever meet – but then they can kind of reveal this almost collective psychosis, and start talking about children being harvested, or lizard people.

And it’s because they never see outside of the media bubble, everything there is kind of waiting to pop. That is kind of the American experience in a way.

> American history has always been about conquest and isolation, in a way.

Absolutely. I think ultimately, Americans, the decay of Americana, in general, is fascinating; because America has always had this kind of hubris to it, where it’s under the assumption that everything can and will be conquered and owned, and there’s like a level of entitlement, that a lot of Americans feel, to their spaces or their land, private property, what have you.

✹

Our country was built around this idea of manifest destiny, you know, where it’s kind of understood that we were “ordained by god” to own and conquer this land, and that delusion was built on a lot of hubris, delusion, and prosecution.

American pride is still very prevalent – it’s considered ridiculous not to be proud, but all it takes to experience the United States is seeing the level of marginalization, and seeing these places where people have been left to rot, literally.

✹

Like, it sounds depressing and pessimistic, and I don’t think my work is depressive or pessimistic, at least from my perspective; it can maybe be for some people, but the thing that, what I’m aiming to document is kind of like, this is indeed part of the American experience.

I think that America is kind of like, a monster that I love, in a weird way. I think that it’s really beautiful. I’m just documenting a phenomenon, something that would exist independently of me trying to record it.

Before I used to put a lot of effort into kind of, the fine art part of my work, by constructing these images, but nowadays, I’m more interested in just finding the weirdest place possible, where the photos are going to turn out in the most unique way – just because it’s real.

The stories are already out there. All of takes is a bit of digging, and a lot of driving (laughs).

> Have you ever struggled with going down a more commercial, easily accessible approach to photography?

That’s been my biggest difficulty – finding like a voice for my work, or finding like clients for my work, you know.

I don’t do this to make money, thankfully, because I probably wouldn’t make very much (laughs), I do it because I think it’s very important for me, and if it were not for it, I don’t know what I would be doing otherwise.

It’s all I do, it’s all I’m interested in doing, and I think that, when it comes to client work, I’ve found some success in creative direction for campaigns, album campaigns for musicians; I think that’s the most fun, because I’m kind of interpreting their artistry, and then I’m kind of flushing it out a little bit more through my pictures.

Telling stories, in general, is really enjoyable for me. I’ve worked for a bunch of publications where I’ve shot journalistic photography; I just documented a number of farmers that were suing the supreme court – that was a very eye-opening experience, and I had a great time doing that.

I can do fine art, and I can document things; and while sometimes I feel that I may be sabotaging myself, and shooting myself in the foot, by trying to do both, I think that’s where the best stories lie. So I'm trying to find a way between the two. I think that is viable.

I’m trying my best to diversify, and I think my work could go both ways.

Ultimately, I’m not doing this for anyone but myself. That’s one thing that I’ve recognized now, especially when I fight myself sometimes for not pursuing a more commercial lane of photography.

I’m not doing this for that, I’m not doing this for money, I’m not looking for it to go viral, like, it’s me. It’s for me, I like these images, I think these are special, and I think these are worth looking at.

If it appeals to an audience or it appeals to people, that’s amazing, but ultimately, I think that is secondary.

If I didn’t do it for me, my work wouldn’t be as – I wouldn’t be as happy with it, and it wouldn’t appeal to as many people probably, ironically, because I think that that’s what it is, there’s a small subset of people that say, I like these stories, the macabre, the bizarre, the surreal of it.

Are you hopeful for the future, creatively speaking?

I feel lucky to have picked a medium that can encapsulate all of my drive and all of my passion. I can always do photography, wherever I am, whatever I’m doing.

When I travel outside of the United States, it’s kind of like, a relief. I can just like, feel the vibe, relax myself. In contrast to that, the downside of traveling the United States, is that I’m always like – we’re going somewhere really pretty, and I will jump like “wait, I think I just saw like, an abandoned, rotting house over there” (laughs) you know?

I would like to keep doing a bit more cultural experiences, and seeing a bit more of the world. I hike a lot, and that’s kind of my new thing. I recently bought a house, I’m gardening, and I think that, as I’m getting older, I’m starting to realize that I’m enjoying the ebb and flow of it.

Before I was much more like, “photography is -the thing-, I’m honing in, and every day I’m working on it”, and nowadays I feel like the work gets way more enjoyable when you have something else that you can of like, you know, diverge into.

That’s what my day job is like, because I can just like work from home, and just chill out, and not everything has to be the “magnum opus”. I feel like I am a lot more passionate about my work now, compared to when I started out, and I think that’s great.

I started photography when I was sixteen, and as I’m growing up, and becoming an adult, I’m only getting more passionate about it. I’m finding more things to be passionate about. I take that as a good sign.
