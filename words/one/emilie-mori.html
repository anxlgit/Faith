möri

This interview was initially conducted in French, and then transcribed, translated, and edited in English.

> How would you introduce yourself and your work to somebody who didn’t know you?

My name is Émilie Mori, and I create oniric and surrealist photographic compositions, which seek to represent the emotions of dance and femininity through collage and movement.

i want my images to be impossible to place within the confines of time.

Initially, I tried to reach this same goal through a more classical illustrative approach; but I couldn’t quite capture a number of things and emotions in the way that I wanted. Photography allowed me to communicate my ideas better.

I also discovered the art of photographic collage, which I didn’t know, thanks to an artist called Brooke Shaden, and immediately loved it.

I loved that method because it allowed me to compose images and thoughts within my own head, like a canvas, before actually taking the pictures. I’m not a passive spectator to my work, I like to create and design the images myself; I like journalistic images, but it’s not something I’m interested in myself, from a creative standpoint.

> The human body always seems to find a way to the heart of your images.

I prefer working with portraits – in fact, I’m very interested in the practice of portraits, especially when the subject is anonymous.

Of course, there are people within my images, because I love working with people, bodies and faces, even when the face itself is obscured by the model’s hair, or some other thing.

I’m very interested in human emotions, and I usually like to put people within a larger natural space.

✹

I'm very close to nature, but I often find that nature itself is not enough for my projects.

there needs to be a human element to it, human emotion, for the image to become whole.

✹

I try to make it easy for the viewer to find him or herself within the image – to project themselves into the image. If the model has a face, whether masculine or feminine, the viewer immediately sees it as someone else; but when subjects have no face, I have the impression that it’s easier for us to imagine that we are indeed within the frame.

That’s why I usually like to obscure faces, but it’s also because I’m very drawn to this concept of atemporality and timelessness.

I want my images to be impossible to place within the confines of time. I don’t want there to be any faces or articles of clothing, decorations, or any other elements that may give up the date of the images.

I want a viewer from 10 years down the line to be able to see the pictures, without placing them within a single point in time. And sometimes, faces themselves allude to a certain period, a trend, or a style. I want to stay as neutral as possible.

> Movement also has a big weight within your images. Why is that?

For me, there’s a very important thing to consider, when we talk about movement, and it’s the fact that it can represent and express many different things at once. It can speak of love, struggle, survival, escape – there’s something quite strong and emotional about movement alone, both culturally and socially.

That’s why I love dance, and I find it to be a really useful tool within photography.

✹

i can take up to two hundred pictures, trying to capture a specific movement.

it goes beyond beautiful; i find there to be something almost spiritual about movement.

✹

>  Do you see any link between the themes of collage and movement?

I usually see it as two different trends within my work, so they don’t necessarily intertwine with each other – I often don’t incorporate any movement within my collage images, because I see it as unnecessary, if the combination of elements already captures what I want to say.

And it’s the same with the other side: I don’t usually seek to add anything to my movement images, because when I manage to capture a specific movement within an image, it almost transports me, you know?

In those cases, I don’t seek to add or remove anything else, because I find the image to be enough as it is, and the fact of being able to capture that movement, that’s magic in and out of itself.

i allow myself to work with my images in the best way i see fit.

But if an image speaks to it, I’ll make a collage to it, regardless to it having movement or not. I don’t limit myself to a certain style – I allow myself to work with my images in the best way I see fit.

I can work on two or three completely different series with very different ideas and concepts at the same time, I don’t forbid myself from any perspective, it’s really the inspiration that counts, letting it speak, and following the path where it may lead.

> You have a background in design, but nowadays, most of your featured work is photographic in nature

Years ago, I mainly worked as a graphic designer, but around 2012, I started doing photography as a hobby, without really having the pretension of living off of it.

But I found so much happiness by doing it, that I started doing it more and more, and it started taking on a bigger and bigger part of my projects, in relation to design.

There wasn’t any “jump” really to speak of – I just kind of balanced both for the longer part of ten years, focusing more and more on this kind of artistic photography side.

And as the photography side of my work grew, the design side steadily declined.

And it’s continued to do so, because working as a photographer makes me incredibly happy. It motivates me enormously, and it’s a much more creative and free approach to creation, fatigued as I was from working with businesses, and creative briefs, stakeholder decisions, project limitations, and so on. Working as a photographer allows me to work much more freely, and that has made me, quite simply, just happier overall.

> Do you see photography as the ultimate way of creative practice?

In fact, no – there are still moments where I find myself tired of working with computers and cameras, and I just feel the urge to unplug the phone, go somewhere else, and just, you know, paint something with watercolors (laughs).

For me, they are all just ways of expression: be it photography, music, painting, design, you name it. Everything is valuable, especially if it has the power to balance us, help us flourish, align us with ourselves.

We very often find ourselves stressed with everyday life, and I find that the best way for me to de-stress is just to let my creativity flow.

what truly counts is finding some peace.

I encourage creative practice, both for myself and for all my friends and relatives as well. It can take many shapes as well – it can be building some furniture, doing some gardening, there’s no end to it.

What truly counts is finding some peace, and returning to who we are; because often, we lose ourselves within the visions of businesses and clients, which don’t necessarily align with our nature, but which are sometimes necessary, nevertheless.

> You mentioned that improvisation is a big part of your work, but improvising also opens the door to making mistakes.

There are two sides to this. On one side, I find it necessary to say that this happens a lot during my projects; failure is something that happens quite often. And I’m not free of feeling angry sometimes, when a project or an idea doesn’t really work as I wanted it to, but it’s just something that you have to deal with – it happens to everybody, you just need to not take it too personally.

When you work doing photography, you usually end up throwing away most of the pictures that you take. That’s just part of the experience.

It can happen to you as well, that you don’t really notice something being off until you sit down for the editing process; and you realize, at that point, that the shooting didn’t really work.

I’ve lost a lot of work and effort and time because of that, and it can drive you mad sometimes, but it’s not the end of the world, you know. It happens.

But there’s another side to this as well. Sometimes, it’s happened to me, in the midst of a shooting, that I realized that the idea didn’t really work.

I often don’t feel comfortable saying it out loud to the person that I’m taking pictures of, especially considering that it’s someone that I asked to come with me to the location, take time out of his or her day, and so on.

I’m not always mature enough to just say, “hey, let’s just cut this, it ain’t working”, right? (laughs). What I usually do in those cases is that I just get it done, put it in a folder somewhere, and forget about it.

mistakes are not the end of the world.

Sometimes, though, I find those projects later down the line – and they end up working beautifully under a different concept. It takes patience in some of these cases, and thankfully, I’m quite a patient person.

I’ve sometimes worked with pictures that I took over two or three years ago, and they have worked perfectly for current projects.

✹

i'm even of the impression that some images need to go through a process of reflection.

i think that failure is just a part of the creative process. there's no way around it.

✹

> Let’s finish this with a quick view towards the future. What do you see in your future?

At the moment, I’m in a bit of a restructuring process of both my activities and services a bit, in a bit more professional manner, because I’d love to work more with bigger brands, to be involved in bigger commercial projects, doing photography.

I’d love to stop doing design work entirely, and focus completely on my photography, working with brands, but retaining my style. I want to keep working on that towards the end of the year, and for that, I will probably seek some sort of photographic agent.

That’s one side of it. The other side is that I’m all the while working on three different projects at the moment, which have barely anything to do with each other (laughs). One is a really noir, dark study on bodies, both feminine and masculine, a kind of chiaroscuro work, studying different parts of bodies, nudity, and so on, trying to achieve this look and feel of ancient paintings.

There’s another one that has to do with a more surrealist, close-up, anonymous subject, closing the camera on small details, parts of the face, mixing different elements in close contact with each other.

And finally, there’s a last project that has to do with water, very long stoles, and movement within water. I’ve always wanted to work with water, because I think it gives movement a different weight, a different feel, and I’m always looking forward to new ways of exploring movement.

So I’m looking forward to all these three projects, and ready for whatever else may come.
