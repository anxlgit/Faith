Álvaro
Arregui

This interview was initially conducted in Spanish, and then transcribed, translated, and edited in English.

> How would you introduce yourself to somebody who didn’t know you and your work?

This is probably the hardest part of my job: just explaining to other people what it is that I do (laughs).

I very often end up just introducing myself as a “designer”; but just by saying that, you’re already opening a can of worms, just by itself. Because that description can easily just go down to “oh, yeah, what is it that you design? Is it industrial, graphic”, and so on, because I think that the concept of design itself is quite large.

I like to bring the ideas from my clients' heads, all the way to reality.

So it depends on who’s asking, or if they already know my work, or kind of know a bit about the industry. If we’re speaking about somebody that has never heard of user interfaces and user experience, I will just point to the applications on their phone, and tell them that I work on that sort of thing. But then they’ll ask me, “ah, so you do websites?” and I’ll be like, “well, no” (laughs).

If we’re speaking about somebody who already knows a bit about the field, it’s as easy as just saying that I do “digital product design”, and they’ll figure it out. I may then have to introduce some concepts about UX, and interfaces, and so on, but that’s about it.

The thing is also that, because of my background, I have a lot of experience in a lot of different fields, but I’m not really an expert in any given one – so what I usually focus on is the process of helping businesses along the whole way, from the beginning phases to the launch of the final product; which, thankfully, is the kind of work that I enjoy doing the most.

✹

I like to think of myself as an orchestra conductor: somebody that may not know how to play all of the different instruments, but that has a special affinity to know how to best make them shine within the composition.

I’m always trying to say, “okay, who’s the end user, what is the problem, are there any other solutions,  what can we learn from them?”

✹

Most of my time nowadays, let’s say 70% of it, is spent working with Tonari, which was one of the startups that I helped start about five years ago; and bit by bit, I ended up becoming a core member within, let’s say, the inner circle.

I’ve been involved in most product areas: from branding, to experience design, product flows, what you see when you use it, what you don’t, working on the know-how, the libraries, you name it.

Right now we’re focusing on the unboxing experience, which could be described as the process that takes place from when you buy the product, until it’s already set up in your own house.

Speaking purely about my graphical style, I majored as a graphic designer, and I’ve always been a sucker for color tiles (laughs). I was that kid that enjoyed technical drawing class way more than, you know, art class.

I loved working with squares and bevels, from very early on, and I also grew up loving cubism, 80s and 90s design, and the Swiss design of the 50s, so there’s a connection there.

Around 2008, I discovered the world of mobile interfaces, and I just jumped head in.

I never liked websites; I always found them to be just a place where products advertised themselves, and I found that to be just boring. But when I discovered interface design, I immediately felt it to be the perfect marriage between graphic design and product design.

I’ve never gone back since then.

> From somebody who describes its work as “reductionist”, your personality seems anything but (laughs). Why do think that is?

Yeah, that’s true (laughs). And I’ve made myself that question innumerable times.

I just started to find an answer to it, after having two children, because one of them is really similar to me, personality-wise, just being a small ball of energy; and I’ve realized, through him, the importance of structure.

I’ve never been a fan of unstructured things, and have always felt this urge to just make things straight. My mother was a cleaner, and some weekends she took me with her to do some cleaning work, and I think that just stuck with me.

At that early age, all of those messages of “do your bed, tidy your room, tuck your shirt in”, just bored you to death. But with the years, I’ve realized that the personality and messages of my mother struck a deeper chord than I initially thought.

In design, simplicity and order are paramount. White space, visual balance, order, hierarchy; those are, you know, the building blocks of the whole thing.

✹

sometimes, I’ve been in a restaurant with a friend, and realized all of a sudden that I had spent some minutes just aligning the cutlery, and other things from the table, because they were not tidy enough (laughs).

When things are not organized, my head is unable to rest. I’ve never liked freehand drawings, graphite, collage work, etc. They make me feel unease.

✹

So, yeah, reductionism is just a part of me. And it’s also useful when dealing with clients; because, you know, having options can be both a blessing and a curse. When you have too many options, choosing becomes difficult, and focusing becomes harder.

Simplicity makes it easier to get to the heart of the experience,  or the product, which is the most important thing. Everything else is just secondary.

That’s also a big reason why I like working in black and white: there are just as many colors as personalities. I’ve heard so many clients to say, “why don’t we try making this element red”, and what ends up happening is that, you start changing things up, and after some time, you end up just where you started – which was the best solution, right from the beginning.

> You currently live and work in Japan, which is quite infamous for its formalism and rigidity. Do you think there may be a connection there?

Of course, no doubt about it. Let me tell you a story – when I started studying design, we were told of a German school of thought and design, called the Bauhaus. I’m sure you’ve heard of it (laughs).

So, yeah, those guys were the precursors of most of the things we’re doing at the moment, whether visually, conceptually, or in terms of philosophy, teaching, you name it.

They cut, as with a sharp razor, the history and tradition of design that came before them, as well as all of the ongoing trends with Art Deco, organic design, decorations, ornaments, and so on.

They wanted to hyper-focus their designs and their work on the basics: functionality, simplicity, and order. Of course, it’s easy to see why I became obsessed with their work.

What I wanted to tell you is that, I once found a letter from one of the founders of the Bauhaus to another; it may have been in an exhibition of their work, or something of the sort, where they spoke about the enormous effect that the Japanese culture had had on their work.

the hierarchy must be respected, everything has to be perfect.

Japan, you know, as a country, opened its gates (or was forced to open them, depending on whom you ask) at around 1853, after staying completely closed off for about 250 years. So, as you can imagine, the Japanese culture that came out of that was unlike anything else anyone had ever seen.

In the letter, from whom I think was Walter Gropius, it was written that most of the concepts of modularity, functionality, and efficiency -that the Bauhaus was obsessed over- had already been perfected by the Japanese culture for hundreds of years.

And to this day, you can still feel this – working with Sony, which is a relatively new and innovative company in Japan, you can still feel the need to hyper-focus on the details, the hierarchy must always be respected, everything has to be perfect, and so on.

At the end of the day, culture is nothing but a repetition of method and thought – and Japanese culture is all about precision, elegance, and order. So yeah, it’s easy to see a connection to my work there.

> Let’s switch topics: photography is another one of your personal obsessions.

I’ve always said that photography is my true passion. But I’ve always felt that making money with it would kill that, in a way – so I’ve always seen it as a way of doing what I want.

It’s been very beneficial to me to think of it in that way, and to focus on doing design as a career, and leave photography as a creative outlet.

Nowadays I’ve started taking more and more pictures, and improving their quality tremendously over time; not because I intend to sell them, or to turn professional with it, but because I want to become as good a photographer as possible.

When I went into art school, and started taking pictures, and developing them – I don’t know if you know this, but revealing black and white pictures is a very involved process – you can play with the exposition by letting the images rest within the lab process, so you can play with lights and shadows with that, and focus, and contrast, brightness, and so on.

I loved that process, it absolutely bewitched me, because I saw that even without color, there’s so much that you can express within a single picture.

> You’ve also applied some of your skills as a designer to the photography world, in terms of app development.

I guess you’re talking about BLACK. Well, at some point I thought that, if working as a photographer was not to become a goal, I should find a way to still work with photography, in some way.

So I got the idea of developing an App for phone photography, because at around the time of its development, I was using a Nokia phone with quite a nice, 40Mpx camera, and I had fun taking pictures with it, but I wanted to be able to make them in black and white, and to use the analog filters that I loved from other apps.

i despise trends, tendencies, and fashion.

Long story short, I got together with a work colleague, and developed quite an interesting interface, stripped of anything unnecessary – unobtrusive, reductionist, just like, gesture-based, reducing the number of interactions and touches to the absolute bare minimum.

I wanted to build a product that could be used 10, 20, 50 years from now; are we not using much older cameras nowadays?

So, yeah, we shipped it to Windows Phone, it kind of blew up, Windows wrote to us, featured the App, and so on. And because so many people with iPhones wrote to us as well, asking us to port the application, we ended up doing that as well, we got featured as well, even ended up in an Apple Keynote.

Unfortunately, this past May, after 10 years, we ended up discontinuing the project; but the results, with over 2 million downloads, were astonishing. And I think it all came down to that idea of working towards a design that could be timeless.

I despise trends and tendencies and fashion, I don’t care if we’re talking about cars, or brands, or whatever. That’s why I love such philosophies as that from the Ulm School of Design, where they tried to reduce things to their bare essentials: a chair should have three legs, because if you remove one more, it falls.

> What do you see in your own future?

The older I get, the more I remember how happy I was, being a little boy, just helping my granddad with the garden. I have no patience for plants, unfortunately (laughs) but I’ve realized that most of my time is spent in front of a screen, and I’m growing quite weary of this synthetic world we’re living in.

i would like to understand people a little bit better.

I’d like to create things that keep connecting us, but that do so in a more humane way. That’s why I love working at Tonari, where our main design goal is to make the product as unobtrusive and natural as possible.

Social media nowadays is kind of at fault for creating these bubbles within which we live, and all that artificiality and make-up that, in my view, brings us more apart than together.

And of course, I'd like to keep working in my photography.

✹

When you do portraits, and you get together with someone else, you talk about a lot of things, anything, and end up knowing quite a bit about each other.

I love that – that feeling of sitting down and talking, and knowing each other, quite like what we used to do in Spain, just getting the chairs out, sitting on the street, talking to each other.

✹

Right now, I do most of my portrait photography with friends – but at some point, I’d love to do this more with strangers, with people I look up to.

I want them to become my friends through this process of mutual discovery; and of course, to part with a portrait that could signify that moment.

I’ve also had other ideas – I’ve toyed recently with the idea of building a salt company. Just salt. There are not many things as fundamental as salt.

Salt is primordial, and unavoidable. And of course, the packaging would be simple, and reductionist: just a sheet of paper, wrapped together with an elastic band.
