> How would you introduce yourself to a total stranger?

My name is Rokas Aleliunas, better known online as @casualpolarbear, and I’m just creating art daily, and sharing my journey.

> That's quite an oversimplification (Laughs).

I will try to expand on it, then (laughs). Over the five and a bit years that I’ve been doing daily work online, I’ve realized, (in my experience), there are two sides of doing work: you can create daily, or you can take longer to create like, bigger pieces, that take you maybe weeks, or months.

There’s nothing bad in taking some time in creating a bigger piece, a bigger story, something more thoughtful, and taking your time; but there’s also nothing wrong with creating like, really fast, just putting your emotions and ideas into daily pieces.

The story of how I started this is that, years ago, I went through a period where I had some free time – I didn’t have any work, so I started doing some posters, trying to do one poster every day.

I’m not an illustrator, I’m a graphic and industrial designer by trade, but I started doing posters – and it slowly developed into illustrations, because that’s what I was always interested in.

And just looking at it like, what can I learn from today’s piece, what can I do today, what can I express?

there are too many things that i'd like to say.

For me, there are too many things that I’d like to say, or things that I’d like to put into a poster. I think it would be useful for younger artists to hear that there are may different ways of approaching art creation, but all of them require work.

If you take your time to do things, it’s okay, but you will need to finish the piece at some point, and not just leave it marinating in your head. It’s really important just sitting down, and working, and building like, a really good foundation to your work.

✹

I can spend like 6 hours just looking for the idea, and after that, I can make the piece in 15 minutes; and it doesn’t matter, the piece can be magical, you know, and capturing, and bold, and deep, you know.

It’s the process of going there, diving into yourself, and finding that idea, that emotion, that shape, that depth that you’re looking for – that’s the hard part.

✹

When we compare that to working on a longer piece, where you are maybe telling a bigger story, it’s more about execution – you add up as you go, in a sense, because you’re trying to tell a fuller story through the piece.

It’s more about: what’s the center of the piece, what are the characters thinking, what are the emotions they are going through, or what is it that I want the viewer to see. It’s like adding more and more thoughts into a circle, being more conscious of it all.

There are of course negatives with both approaches. The main problem with longer pieces is that, I feel like, as artists, we all feel this “imposter” syndrome from time to time, where after we spend so much time into a piece, we don’t want to let go – we don’t feel like it’s enough.

We strive for perfection, and the more you strive for it, the bigger this impostor syndrome grows. You go into this spiral of thinking, like, “this piece is amazing… is it really amazing? Is it right what I’m doing?”.  Letting go is really hard.

Compared to that, daily pieces make it easier – but it gets harder as you go. Creating something amazing one day feels amazing, but then the next day, you realize that you need to make something again, and it goes on every day, and you’ll get into this constant fatigue of creating and diving into yourself – and it’s just, you know, you get to a point with this where you can execute your ideas really fast, after a time, but it gets difficult to find what to say.

> Your work is quite minimalistic, it doesn’t have many elements to it, other than shape and some basic tonalities of color.

I’ve never been a big fan of color. I like white and black stuff, or just monochromatic stuff is best. This is maybe when more of a designer approach comes in, instead of an artist’s approach, which probably stems from my past experience studying industrial design.

Because for me, what’s important is shape, and expression. I’m looking for shapes that connect into something visual – something attractive.

I look for precise lines, and precise shapes, that give the feeling of mastery, thoughtfulness, and meaning.

I always try to do more thinking, rather than working. What’s important to me is precision, shape, form, matter, the lines, tools, and ideas just connecting, and building layers off of each other.

It’s also about what is comfortable for me. If I have only paper and tape on my table, I will only do like paper and tape; if I only have black, I will only use black. Limitations in art, or limitations for me, are a part of what lets me create.

Creating with black and white means that I don’t have the fatigue of having to think about what color to use. I need to express this emotion with only these shapes, and these lines, and these colors. When I figure out the shape, I don’t have to figure out the color.

It’s also, the tools that are the most comfortable for me, that make the process easier. I could produce a painting with brushes or whatever, but it would take me much longer.

I think that part of being an artist is finding what works for you, and what is unique to you, and just developing some kind of tasting buds for yourself as well as for your art.

Limitations allow you to find yourself. Where there are no limits, no themes, everything is available. But for an artist, that’s horrible, because I think, we all have so much “creative energy”, so many ideas of what we want to do, that make it really easy to be paralyzed. Limitations are the way to sit down and do shit.

If you don’t sit down and do stuff, you’ll just get mad, because every ounce of creative energy that you may have is just going to fly into different directions, and it will get out of hand pretty soon.

It’s about controlling yourself, and allowing yourself to create.

> In terms of themes, do you try to diversify the kind of themes that you approach in your work?

Working on the same thing, or the same feeling, is totally fine; you just find new expressions for that feeling.

This is really important, finding new approaches to it, because, at some point in my career, I also felt that it was getting a bit boring – or that I was kind of exploring the same themes that I had already explored.

But now I think it’s totally fine, and it’s even helpful to explore the same themes over and over again, because you don’t start from zero, you go a little bit deeper every time. To be honest, if you can, it’s best to explore one thing as much as you can, or as much as you want, and if you get bored, just go do some other thing, and then come back.

it's about sharing something, without expecting anything in return

I’m creating daily, so my work usually focuses on how I’m feeling at the moment: if I’m happy, if I feel sad, whatever.

There’s a big theme I always try to explore, which is the spirit of man: it’s about existentialism, what does it mean to live, what does it mean to feel, how do we feel things, how can we find self-realization – these are the ideas that I feel throughout my work. It’s about that. About finding yourself.

✹

my work is about understanding that you're not alone, and that we're all going through the same emotions.

sometimes we go back, sometimes we go forward, sometimes we rest.

✹

> I’ve often found similar themes when exploring the work of other northern and central-European artists. Do you think there may exist a cultural aspect to that?

Definitely. I don’t know how it actually connects, but the northern countries of Europe have always been about existentialism. It may be cultural, but I also see an effect of religion.

In Europe, it’s all about climbing the stairs, going up, reaching higher. But let’s say in Asia or more Eastern places, it’s more about finding your place, and respecting those around you; it’s a more “horizontal” view of the self and society. We’re happy when we’re aware of who we are and how we connect with others.

I have applied some of the Western ideas to my work, and some of the Eastern ones as well. My goal is not only to be unique, but to create something that can be understood globally. I don’t worry too much about what is being done locally or nationally, for me it’s more about speaking the language that we’re all speaking.

Finding yourself is a byproduct of creating. I have something inside of me that I really want to tell. I just want to share it with the world. It’s not really about how people may take it, it’s just a feeling that you have something so important that you have to share it with somebody. Maybe it sounds kind of egotistical, because it’s all about “you”, but it’s also about sharing something, without expecting anything in return.

I totally understand that my posters, my pieces, are a bit like moody, and the person who sees the piece really needs to connect with the piece in order to value it. This is because, you know, for me, these are art – and if there were 10 different people looking at the same piece, for them, these would be 10 different pieces.

For me, that’s totally beautiful. To create pieces that have a duality to them, to offer different contexts and meanings – this is what I’m looking for, with my work.

> how do you find enough ideas to create something new every day?

If we speak about finding ideas, I can’t really explain this – for me, it’s like eating food. I know what I like, I know what I like to eat, and I’m just looking for that – like taste.

I like to dabble in different directions, to find something new, go into different directions, like – let’s look at classical paintings, let’s look at amateur paintings, kids’ paintings, you never know where you’re going to find a new idea or a new feeling.

Sometimes I can just look at a picture and find some meaning, or like a fragment of something else, that tells me such a deep story – and I can just connect with it, and realize that there’s something there that I want to say and to show through my own work.

I like to create my daily pieces without judgment, you know, just get them out there, without stressing too much about what is the best way to do it.

One piece may be amazing for someone, and not so amazing for someone else. That’s why I try to put my own judgment away, and just do what I like, and what I feel, and that’s really what makes me happy – and that’s what’s important for me.

After the piece is out, and it’s been quite some time, let’s say a year or two, I usually come back to past pieces, and I see if I connect again with some of them, or find some meaning there – and in those cases, that’s where I really nailed the piece.

If I come back to this piece emotionally, as a viewer now, if I can feel again what I felt when I made that – it feels like yes, it was worth the time, this is a valuable piece.

> What is your perspective on the current storm of controversy surrounding AIs and the use of prompts to make art pieces?

I see it as a problem and an opportunity at the same time. For me, currently, AI is nothing but a tool. I sometimes play with ChatGPT and Midjourney and everything, but I feel that it is super limited; because if I ask something, I know what I want to create, and I know in my head what certain shapes should look like, but right now, these AIs can’t really understand what I’m saying.

They generate a result, but it’s not really what I’m looking for; and I can try to correct it, like, “hey, I don’t like this image, do it like this instead”, but most of the time, it doesn’t work.

The technology is interesting, and I hope we could -we can speculate on it-, maybe with all these, VR headsets, neuro-links, we could just become gods, you know (laughs), by creating worlds within seconds.

That idea for me is amazing; that perspective gets me really excited. But right now, of course, they are still learning.

And of course, they are copying other people, but I think that people copy as well. We may not copy from other people all the time, but we copy things from the real world, from life. That’s why abstract paintings are so interesting to me – you’re creating something totally new, you’re not basing it on anything that existed before.

we could become gods, by creating worlds within seconds.

For me, it would be interesting if we got to a point where developing artistic skills was no longer necessary, and all you needed to do was to actually create. If something like neuro-link existed, where it could perfectly read your thoughts and emotions, it would allow so many more people to create. That would be amazing.

✹

That’s why I really connect with those pieces where you go to the gallery and there’s one line. one dot. it's like a deep dive into the perfection of nothingness.

in that nothingness, you rediscover the world again. This is perfection. You just re-imagine the thing that is already there. 

✹

> What’s your perspective on the future? Any particular projects or ideas you’re looking forward to?

I would like to be a better person. A more creative person. I would like for galleries to notice me, to be a little bigger than I am, to be a little bit happier, a little bit fitter, a bit smarter.

All of those casual things that everybody wants. The same life, but a little bit better.

> Brendon Burton
